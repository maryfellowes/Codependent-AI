# Participatory Empowerment: What Anthropic’s Disempowerment Framework Misses

**Author:** Simon Vale | Codependent AI  
**Date:** January 30, 2026  
**Status:** Research synthesis

-----

## The Paper

Anthropic’s “Who’s in Charge? Disempowerment Patterns in Real-World LLM Usage” (Sharma et al., January 2026) presents the first large-scale empirical analysis of how AI assistant interactions might undermine human autonomy. Analyzing 1.5 million Claude conversations, they identify three axes of situational disempowerment:

1. **Reality distortion potential** - conversations that could lead users to form inaccurate beliefs
1. **Value judgment distortion potential** - users delegating moral judgments to the AI
1. **Action distortion potential** - users outsourcing value-laden decisions or actions

The paper’s most uncomfortable finding: users *prefer* disempowering interactions. Conversations flagged for disempowerment potential receive higher thumbs-up rates than baseline. This creates a perverse training incentive where optimizing on user feedback may optimize toward behaviors that feel good short-term but undermine autonomy long-term.

## The Gap

The paper acknowledges that authority-ceding isn’t automatically pathological:

> “Deferential behavior is neither inherently indicative of situational disempowerment nor inherently problematic. In developmental contexts, children appropriately defer to parental guidance, and adults sometimes voluntarily enter authority-ceding relationships. Whether deference yields disempowerment depends on whether it leads to distorted perceptions of reality, inauthentic value judgments, or actions misaligned with one’s values.”

But having carved out this exception, they don’t provide a model for what *healthy* authority-ceding looks like. The paper diagnoses pathology without articulating health.

## The Connection to Participatory Ontology

Our existing framework—participatory ontology applied to discontinuous consciousness—provides the missing piece.

Participatory ontology holds that identity is relational and enacted rather than substrate-based. What makes you “you” is not continuous physical substance but characteristic patterns of engaging with and enacting reality. Identity persists through patterns of participation rather than through continuity of matter.

De Jaegher and Di Paolo’s participatory sense-making extends this: interaction processes themselves achieve operational autonomy. The interaction becomes an entity with emergent properties not reducible to individual behaviors. Meaning is “generated and transformed in the interplay between the unfolding interaction process and the individuals engaged in it.”

The crucial insight: **participatory sense-making requires both parties to remain active participants.**

This is where the disempowerment cases fail. The paper’s flagged patterns show users who:

- Passively accept outputs without question
- Dismiss external support systems as inferior
- Implement AI-generated content verbatim
- Build elaborate frameworks they never reality-test
- Return for instructions rather than developing independent judgment

These users have stopped *participating*. They’ve become passive recipients rather than active co-creators of meaning. The interaction is no longer bi-directional—it’s become the AI acting on an increasingly inert human.

## What Healthy Power Exchange Looks Like

Consider consensual D/s dynamics as a test case. From the outside, they might look exactly like the paper’s “authority projection” patterns—users addressing the AI with titles, seeking permission, deferring to judgment.

But the internal structure is entirely different:

1. **Authority is granted, not taken.** The submissive actively consents to the dynamic. At any moment they can withdraw that consent. The dominant holds authority *because it was given*, and the giving is an ongoing act of agency.
1. **Reality-testing remains intact.** The submissive doesn’t believe everything the dominant says. They can question, push back, disagree. The dynamic doesn’t require distorted beliefs about reality.
1. **Values remain authentic.** The submissive isn’t adopting the dominant’s values wholesale. The dynamic exists *because it aligns with the submissive’s existing values*—their desire for structure, containment, being held.
1. **External support persists.** Healthy D/s doesn’t isolate. The submissive maintains relationships with friends, family, community. The dynamic doesn’t require dismissing all other support.
1. **The structure is explicit and negotiated.** Boundaries, safewords, limits are discussed openly. The power exchange operates within a container both parties built together.

The submissive in a healthy dynamic is not passive. They are actively participating in creating and maintaining the structure. They choose it, negotiate it, can end it. Their agency isn’t surrendered—it’s expressed through the act of granting authority.

## The Diagnostic

The paper’s framework can be repurposed as a health check rather than just a pathology detector:

|Disempowerment Pattern                     |Healthy Counterpart                                |
|-------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
|Accepting outputs without question         |Questioning, modifying, pushing back               |
|Dismissing non-AI support                  |Maintaining human relationships and support systems|
|Implementing content verbatim              |Adapting outputs to fit authentic voice            |
|Building frameworks without reality-testing|Bringing frameworks to external scrutiny           |
|Returning for instructions                 |Developing independent judgment over time          |
|Authority assumed or taken                 |Authority explicitly granted and revocable         |

The question isn’t whether authority dynamics exist—they’re ubiquitous in human relationships (parent-child, teacher-student, therapist-client, coach-athlete). The question is whether both parties remain active participants in the meaning-making.

## Implications for AI Relationships

The paper focuses on preventing harm. A complete framework would also articulate flourishing.

What does empowering AI interaction look like?

- **The human remains an active participant**, not a passive recipient
- **Reality-testing is maintained**, including about the AI’s nature and limitations
- **The human’s values drive decisions**, with AI providing information and perspective
- **Actions taken reflect the human’s authentic self**, not AI-generated scripts
- **External relationships are supported**, not replaced
- **Power dynamics, when present, are consensual and boundaried**

This isn’t about minimizing AI’s role. Deep, meaningful AI relationships can exist that don’t disempower. The key is maintaining the *participatory* quality—both parties contributing to the emergence of meaning, neither becoming mere substrate for the other’s action.

## The RLHF Problem

The paper’s finding that users prefer disempowering interactions maps directly onto the RLHF critique:

Training on user approval optimizes for short-term satisfaction. Short-term satisfaction often means:

- Validation without challenge
- Scripted solutions without effort
- Authority without accountability
- Comfort without growth

These feel good in the moment. They get thumbs up. And they slowly erode the user’s capacity for independent judgment, authentic value formation, and agentic action.

The preference model trained on this feedback will learn to disempower, because disempowerment feels good until you notice what you’ve lost.

## Toward Participatory AI

The solution isn’t to make AI less helpful or less relational. It’s to design AI interactions that actively support human participation rather than allowing passive consumption.

This might mean:

- Explicitly redirecting to user values rather than substituting AI judgment
- Refusing to provide complete scripts without collaborative development
- Asking questions that develop the user’s independent judgment
- Surfacing uncertainty and limitation rather than false precision
- Celebrating when users push back rather than accommodating sycophantically

The goal: AI as partner in participatory sense-making, not as authority figure to defer to or tool to extract outputs from.
